title: "Promises and Perils: Women's Empowerment After Revolution"
collection: publications
status: "Dissertation and Book Project"
abstract: "My dissertation and book project addresses a question that has been overlooked in both revolution and gender scholarship: why do revolutionary regimes diverge so sharply in their treatment of women’s rights? While 20th-century social revolutions all claimed to remake the political and social order, some produced sweeping gender reforms while others retrenched or remained silent. Existing theories emphasize modernization, ideology, or women’s presence in institutional junctures, but none explain this wide variation. My project offers a new answer: the distinctive role of anticolonial mobilization in shaping revolutionary agendas. Anticolonial social revolutions---those that simultaneously dismantle colonial domination and entrenched class hierarchies---are especially likely to produce durable gains for women. By reframing women’s subordination as a product of colonial domination, anticolonial social revolutions linked women’s liberation to national sovereignty, mobilized broader support, fostered solidarities and ideological commitments that disrupted colonial legacies, expanded claims to equality, and embedded gender reforms into state institutions through single-party governance and mass organizations. Methodologically, this project combines large-N causal inference, comparative historical analysis, computational text analysis, and extensive archival and interview evidence from over six months of in-depth fieldwork in Cuba. Cross-national statistical analysis reveals that anticolonial social revolutions yielded the largest and most durable gains in women’s political empowerment compared to other revolutionary types, and were uniquely likely to maintain elite cohesion through single-party governance. Importantly, while revolutions that were both social and anticolonial evolved into single-party regimes, neither socialism nor anticolonialism alone proves sufficient to generate significant gains for women. To probe mechanisms, I compare the 1959 Cuban Revolution with Mexico’s failed socialist revolution (1917)."
