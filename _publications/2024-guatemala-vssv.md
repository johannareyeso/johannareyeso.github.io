title: "Reducing Violence Against Women: Evidence from Specialized Services in Guatemala"
collection: publications
status: "Under Review"  
abstract: "When do state interventions make legal rights enforceable in weak institutional environments? States have implemented diverse strategies to address gender-based violence, which affects at least one-third of women worldwide. While studies of justice system interventions have yielded mixed results, the impact of health-based programs remains less understood. Drawing on qualitative fieldwork, I argue that the primary mechanism through which health-based interventions exert their impact is enforcement signaling: clinics coordinate inter-institutional responses that convey credible enforcement efforts. I evaluate the Guatemalan government's network of specialized sexual violence clinics designed to jointly activate a medical and judicial response. Using original panel data on crimes and prosecutions and a difference-in-differences design that exploits temporal and spatial variation in clinic rollout, I find that access to clinics significantly reduces sexual violence and increases crime prosecution. Descriptive evidence of reduced social tolerance of gender-based violence, particularly among women, suggests that clinics may also shift community attitudes and empower victims and survivors. The study advances comparative debates on state capacity and enforcement, showing how rights-based service delivery can reconfigure the politics of protection in low-capacity states.
